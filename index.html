To start off, let us explain how we harvested these spices.

Peppercorn\Pepper

Pepper grows on climbing vines. When the tiny green berries ripen, they turn red. We carefully handpick them, then dry them in the sun until they transform into the black peppercorns that you find in curry and other indian dishes. You can also put it on omlettes (important for protein) & pasta (which we love).
Another fun fact is that pepper is one of the most globally popular condiments, however, salt may be more popular.

Nutmeg

Nutmeg grows on a short tree, it starts out yellow and when ripe, splits open to reveal the nutmeg seed, surrounded by a delicate, red covering known as mace. After carefully harvesting the seeds, we dry them until they become the familiar nutmeg spice that is used in everything from curries to desserts.

Let me tell you why we grow our own spices at home.

Growing spices at home enhances food security, health, and sustainability. Cultivating spices like pepper and nutmeg ensures a reliable, organic source of high-quality ingredients, reducing dependence on external and inflated markets. These spices provide nutritional and medicinal benefits, supporting better health, while local growth reduces carbon footprint, promoting environmental sustainability. Overall, it nurtures a more resilient and eco-conscious food system.

Harvesting and grinding Peppercorns?

1- Ripening stage

Peppercorns grow in green clusters and ripen over time. They start off as green, then turn yellow, and eventually become red when fully ripe. The timing of harvesting depends on the type of pepper (black, white, or green) you want to produce.

For black pepper, (Which is what we harvested) black pepper is harvested when the peppercorns are still green but have started to turn slightly yellow. At this stage, the berries are just beginning to ripen but are not fully mature.

2- Harvesting-

The process involves carefully picking the peppercorn clusters by hand, usually from tall vines. Farmers use ladders or long poles to reach higher parts of the plant. It's important to avoid damaging the vines and unripe peppercorns during this process.

This is one of the trees we got peppercorn for you from, we used a ladder to take out some of the fruits

Processing

For black pepper, the harvested green berries are dried in the sun or using mechanical dryers. This drying process shrinks the berries, darkens the skin, and gives the peppercorns their characteristic wrinkled appearance.

Grinding-

Since the peppercorns have just been sun-dried, store them in an airtight container to preserve the natural, aromatic scent that peppercorns carry. This scent will fade if the peppercorns are exposed to sunlight for too long.

The process of grinding peppercorns is simple. Peppercorns can be grinded using an electric grinder or food processor. Just put your peppercorns in the grinder until they have become pepper. However, there are many different ways peppercorn is grinded. They include-

  • Using a pepper mill, which involves simply placing the dried peppercorns in the mill's chamber, and turning the mechanism to grind them. Most mills allow you to adjust the grind size, from coarse to fine.
  • Using a mortar and pestle involves placing the dried peppercorns in the mortar and crushing them with the pestle by pressing and twisting. This method allows you to control the coarseness of the grind and release the full flavor of the peppercorns.
  • Lastly, as earlier mentioned, one can use an electric grinder or food processor to grind peppercorn.

Nutmeg (Jadi)

This is what a nutmeg tree looks like!

Nutmegs are found growing on lush nutmeg trees. We found these in the same area in which we harvested peppercorn. Nutmeg trees thrive in the humid, tropical regions of Kerala, especially in areas like Idukki, Wayanad, and Kottayam, which are known for their spice plantations.

What is the process of harvesting nutmeg?

Ripening stage-

Nutmeg fruits ripen once they turn a yellow or orange color. This is the time when they are ready for harvest. The fruit is usually harvested when it falls naturally from the tree or can be picked manually by climbing the tree. The ideal time to harvest is when the fruit begins to split open slightly, indicating that it is ripe and the nutmeg seed inside is mature.

Harvesting-

The ripe fruits are carefully harvested by hand. They may fall from the tree naturally when fully ripe, or they can be collected by shaking the tree or using poles to reach the higher branches. After picking, the fruits should be handled gently to avoid damaging the delicate seeds inside.

Extracting nutmeg and mace-

Once harvested, the fruit is cut open to reveal the seed (which will become the nutmeg) and the outer covering (mace). Mace is the reddish, fleshy covering around the seed that is carefully peeled off by hand. This is dried separately. Nutmeg is the seed inside, which is removed from the fruit and cleaned to remove any remaining pulp.

The fruit in your box, up on the tree!

Drying-

For mace, it is spread out in the sun and dried for about 10 to 12 days. As it dries, it turns orange-red and becomes brittle. Once dried, mace is used in cooking or ground into mace powder. The seeds (nutmeg) are dried by sun-drying for about 7-10 days, depending on weather conditions. As they dry, the seeds shrink, and the nutmeg becomes hard and dark brown in color. In some cases, the nutmeg is dried in a well-ventilated area or under controlled conditions using mechanical dryers to speed up the drying process.

This is how green, red, and black pepper dries. (And nutmeg). We laid it out for a couple of days near our pool where we get the most sun

Storage-

Nutmeg seeds are stored in a cool, dry place to maintain their flavor and freshness. Whole nutmeg is preferred over pre-ground nutmeg because it retains its essential oils and flavor for a longer period.

Final product-

The whole dried nutmeg can be sold whole and grated as needed for cooking. Nutmeg can also be ground into powder, which is commonly used as a spice in cooking and baking.
